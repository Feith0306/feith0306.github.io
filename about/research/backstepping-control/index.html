Adaptive Backstepping Control for Complex Nonlinear Systems

This research direction studies how to stabilize and optimize nonlinear systems when the plant contains unknown dynamics, input nonlinearities, output constraints, switching modes, time delays, and adversarial disturbances.

Safety-Constrained Adaptive Neural Control for PMSM Drives

Representative paper: T. Fei et al., Neurocomputing, 2025. [PDF]

Research Problem

The key problem is to control a stochastic permanent magnet synchronous motor (PMSM) when the electrical angular velocity must satisfy asymmetric time-varying safety constraints, the voltage inputs pass through dead-zone with multiple unknown control directions.

Treating these effects separately is insufficient because input nonlinearity, output safety, stochastic disturbance, and uncertain motor parameters interact directly in the backstepping recursion.

Research Method

The method combines adaptive neural backstepping, radial basis function neural networks, a barrier Lyapunov function, and a new Nussbaum-type design. The Nussbaum component decouples multiple unknown control gains, the barrier Lyapunov function enforces the asymmetric time-varying tracking-error constraint, and adaptive compensation laws handle uncertainty, stochastic terms, and dead-zone effects in the q-axis and d-axis voltage channels are settled by the proposed design.

Main Conclusion

The resulting controller keeps all closed-loop signals bounded and guarantees that the angular-velocity tracking error does not violate the prescribed time-varying asymmetric constraints. The theoretical contribution is the integration of safety constraints and input dead-zone compensation into a stochastic PMSM backstepping framework with multiple unknown control directions.

Simulation Results

The simulations use a stochastic PMSM model with explicit q-axis and d-axis dead-zone mappings, a time-varying load disturbance, and a sinusoidal reference signal. The angular velocity tracks the reference under load variation; the constrained tracking error remains inside its time-varying bounds; the transformed error stays within its admissible interval; the q-axis current follows the DSC internal signal; and the d-axis current converges toward the origin. Adaptive signals remain bounded throughout the process.

Distributed Bipartite Synchronization of Switched Heterogeneous Multi-Agent Systems

Representative paper: T. Fei et al., Journal of the Franklin Institute, 2026. [PDF]

Research Problem

The problem is to achieve bipartite synchronization for heterogeneous nonlinear multi-agent systems over a signed graph when each follower may have asynchronous switching modes, multiple time-varying state delays, uncertain nonlinear dynamics, and input dead-zone nonlinearities. These features make standard consensus or bipartite synchronization protocols for linear systems inadequate.

Research Method

The method develops a distributed adaptive protocol using RBF neural approximation, command filters, a two-layer compensation mechanism, and Lyapunov-Krasovskii functionals with average dwell time. The first compensation layer attenuates command-filter errors and avoids repeated differentiation of virtual controls, while the second high-order layer compensates input dead-zone effects. The Lyapunov-Krasovskii and ADT construction handles asynchronous switching and time-varying delays in a unified stability analysis.

Main Conclusion

The closed-loop analysis establishes uniform ultimate boundedness of all signals and practical bipartite synchronization. The method preserves a local information structure: each follower uses its own states and neighbor information rather than a centralized coordinator, which is important for scalable networked systems.

Simulation Results

The numerical example considers one leader and four heterogeneous followers with different system orders, switching modes, nonlinear dynamics, time-varying delays, and dead-zone input mappings. The results show the switching signals, control inputs, and leader-follower state trajectories. Followers in the two signed groups synchronize to opposite leader trajectories, indicating successful bipartite synchronization. The average-dwell-time analysis also explains how switching frequency is bounded over the simulation horizon.

Robust H∞ Tracking for Strict-Feedback Nonlinear Systems

Representative paper: T. Fei et al., International Journal of Robust and Nonlinear Control, 2026. [PDF]

Research Problem

The problem is to design robust tracking controller for nonlinear strict-feedback systems affected by finite-energy disturbances. A purely bounded-disturbance treatment is conservative for many physical systems, while directly solving the Hamilton-Jacobi-Isaacs equation is generally intractable for nonlinear strict-feedback dynamics.

Research Method

The method reformulates robust H∞ tracking as a zero-sum game between a minimizing controller and a maximizing disturbance. It combines dynamic surface backstepping with Actor-Critic-Disturbance learning. The value function is decomposed into a quadratic tracking component and a nonlinear residual component, and the experience-replay-assisted sufficiency-of-excitation condition improves adaptive weight convergence without relying on the classical persistence-of-excitation requirement.

Main Conclusion

The method provides a bridge between Lyapunov-based robust backstepping and data-driven game-theoretic optimization. It yields stable closed-loop tracking, approximates the Nash equilibrium between controller and worst-case disturbance, and avoids directly solving the HJI equation.

Simulation Results

Two simulations validate the framework. In the numerical strict-feedback example, the states track their reference trajectories under approximate worst-case disturbances, and the Actor and Disturbance weights remain bounded. In the practical one-link robot-arm example, the link angle tracks a sinusoidal reference under finite-energy disturbance, the phase portrait converges, tracking and filter errors decrease, and control/disturbance inputs remain well behaved. Larger design gains improve tracking transients in the reported comparison.

Summary and Significance

This research direction builds a coherent control framework for complex nonlinear systems.

  • The Neurocomputing work focuses on safety-constrained adaptive control of a servo-drive system.
  • The JFI work extends adaptive backstepping to multi-agent systems under switching and delay effects.
  • The IJRNC work further connects backstepping with robust H∞ learning.

Taken together, the direction moves from single-plant constrained control to networked synchronization and then to adversarial robust learning, showing how backstepping can be combined with neural approximation and game-theoretic learning for practical nonlinear systems.
