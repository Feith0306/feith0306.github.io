Projected Self-Triggered Reinforcement Learning for Differential Games

This research direction studies reinforcement learning for differential games under intermittent execution. The central issue is to reduce sensing, communication, and online computation while preserving stability, robustness, and safe learning dynamics.

Self-Triggered H∞ Learning for Zero-Sum Games

Representative paper: T. Fei et al., IEEE Trans on Emerging Topics in Computational Intelligence, 2026. [PDF]

Research Problem

The problem is to deploy learning-based H∞ zero-sum-game control when continuous trigger monitoring is costly and adaptive Actor/Disturbance weights may drift under online learning. Event-triggered learning reduces updates, but it still requires continuous checking of triggering conditions, which weakens its practical advantage on resource-limited platforms.

Research Method

The method introduces a self-triggered Actor-Critic-Disturbance learning architecture. The next update instant is computed from sampled-state information through a self-triggered rule, then the control input is held by a zero-order hold. Projection operators constrain Actor and Disturbance adaptive weights in compact sets, while current and recorded data are used to improve learning. The closed-loop problem is analyzed under finite-gain L2 stability and Zeno-free execution.

Main Conclusion

The framework eliminates continuous trigger monitoring, confines adaptive weights, and guarantees finite-gain L2 stability under the self-triggered implementation. The projection mechanism is not cosmetic: without it, weight growth can violate the prescribed H∞ performance requirement.

Simulation Results

The simulations include an F-16 aircraft plant and a nonlinear single-link robot arm. In the F-16 case, all state components are regulated to the origin, and the self-triggered method uses 12 samples over the simulation horizon, compared with 24 for event-triggered control and 500 for time-triggered control. In the robot-arm case, the state trajectories converge to a small neighborhood of the origin, triggering intervals grow as the system stabilizes, projection keeps Actor weights inside the prescribed bound, and the achieved attenuation level stays below the required threshold.

Self-Triggered Fuzzy Reinforcement Learning for Multi-Player Nonzero-Sum Games

Representative paper: T. Fei et al., IEEE Transactions on Fuzzy Systems, 2026. [PDF]

Research Problem

The problem is to learn Nash equilibrium strategies for continuous-time multi-player nonzero-sum differential games. In addition, sparse feedback updates can make Actor-Critic learning vulnerable to parameter drift and insufficiently informative data, especially when persistence of excitation is unavailable.

Research Method

The method embeds fuzzy logic systems into a hybrid Actor-Critic architecture. Critics evolve continuously, while Actors are updated only at self-triggered instants. Projection-based Actor updates keep weights bounded, and experience replay enriches the Bellman-error learning signals under limited sampling. The self-triggered mechanism predicts future update instants from sampled state information and excludes Zeno behavior.

Main Conclusion

The resulting fuzzy Actor-Critic RL algorithm removes continuous state monitoring, guarantees bounded adaptive weights, supports uniform ultimate boundedness of state and learning errors, and reduces cumulative cost through projection and replay-enhanced learning.

Simulation Results

The simulation uses a modified torsional pendulum with three players and an unknown external disturbance. Compared with an event-triggered baseline, the self-triggered scheme uses far fewer triggering instants, 51 versus 356, with a larger average interval and a strictly positive minimum interval. Sensitivity results show that smaller triggering parameters improve regulation at the cost of more samples. State trajectories converge to a small neighborhood of the origin; projected control inputs remain bounded; cost functions decrease faster and settle at lower values with projection; and Actor/Critic online errors remain bounded under disturbances.

Summary and Significance

This research direction targets a practical bottleneck in learning-based control: continuous monitoring is often unrealistic in networked or embedded systems.

  • The IEEE TETCI work addresses zero-sum H∞ robustness through projected self-triggered learning.
  • The IEEE TFS work extends the idea to fuzzy reinforcement learning for nonzero-sum games.

The shared contribution is a safety-aware intermittent-learning framework: self-triggering reduces sampling and communication, projection prevents adaptive-parameter drift, and stored data improves learning under sparse updates.
